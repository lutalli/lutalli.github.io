> <span class="math-h">DEF-TUP. Tuple ($\mathsf{ZF}$).</span>
>
> The **ordered pair** / **2-tuple** / **couple** of two sets $a$ and $b$, written as $(a,b)$, is the set
>
> $$ \{\{a\},\{a,b\}\}. $$
>
> The **3-tuple** / **triple** of three sets $a$, $b$ and $c$ is
>
> $$ (a,b,c) = ((a,b),c). $$
>
> **4-tuples** / **quadruples**, **5-tuples** / **quintuples**, etc. are defined analogously.

> <span class="math-h">REM-TUP.</span>
>
> Notice that the arity is not an intrinsic attribute of a tuple. For example, $(a,b,c)$, the **3-tuple** of $a$, $b$ and $c$, is the **2-tuple** of $(a,b)$ and $c$. Technically, we can not really assert $(a,b)$ is a 2-tuple. We can only say it is the 2-tuple **of $a$ and $b$**.

> <span class="math-h">PROP-TUP.</span>
>
> For any sets $a$, $b$, $a'$ and $b'$,
>
> $$ (a,b)=(a',b') \enspace\Leftrightarrow\enspace a=a'\land b=b'. $$
>
> For any sets $a$, $b$, $c$, $a'$, $b'$ and $c'$,
>
> $$ (a,b,c)=(a',b',c') \enspace\Leftrightarrow\enspace a=a'\land b=b'\land c=c'. $$
>
> Analogous results hold for quadruples, quintuples, etc.
