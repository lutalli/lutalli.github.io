<span class="math-h">Proposition 1.</span>Let $X$, $Y$ be non-empty sets and $R\subseteq X\times Y$ be a relation with domain $X$. $R$ is injective, if and only if

$$ R\left[\bigcap S\right] = \bigcap_{A\in S} R[A] $$

for all $S\subseteq P(X)$.

<span class="math-h">Proof.</span>("$\rightarrow$") Assume $R$ is injective. Let $S\subseteq P(X)$. 
- ("$\subseteq$") Trivial.
- ("$\supseteq$") Let $y\in\bigcap_{A\in S} R[A]$. Let $A\in S$, then there is an $x\in A$ such that $xRy$. But for every $A'\in S$ there is an $x'\in A'$ such that $x'Ry$. Since $R$ is injective, we have $x=x'$, hence $x\in A'$. This shows $x\in\bigcap S$, so $y\in R\left[\bigcap S\right]$.

("$\leftarrow$") Assume

$$ R\left[\bigcap S\right] = \bigcap_{A\in S} R[A] $$

for all $S\subseteq P(X)$. Let $x\in X$, then there is a $y\in Y$ such that $xRy$. Suppose $x'\in X$ and $x'Ry$. Since $\\{x\\}$, $\\{x'\\}\subseteq X$, the assumption gives us

$$ R[\{x\}\cap\{x'\}] = R[\{x\}]\cap R[\{x'\}]. $$

Since $xRy$ and $x'Ry$, we have $y\in R[\\{x\\}]\cap R[\\{x'\\}]$, so $y\in R[\\{x\\}\cap\\{x'\\}]$. That means $\\{x\\}\cap\\{x'\\}$ is not empty, so we must have $x=x'$. Thus $R$ is injective. <span class="math-qed">$\square$</span>
